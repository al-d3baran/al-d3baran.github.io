The Bull's bloodshot eye

Aldebaran is a bright star in the night sky, located in the constellation of Taurus. It's one of the easiest stars to find and is known for its reddish appearance.

It is a red giant star, which means it's larger and cooler than our Sun. It's situated at a distance of approximately 65 light-years from Earth. This star is quite prominent, especially between late November and early December.

At an apparent visual magnitude of 0.86, Aldebaran is one of the top 15 brightest stars in the night sky. In Arabic, Aldebaran means “the Follower”, because it follows the bright Pleiades star cluster as it rises in the night.

It has a fascinating history in various cultures and myths, making it a popular celestial object for both ancient and modern stargazers.

Red giant's facts

  • Name Origin:The name Aldebaran comes from the Arabic "الدبران" (Nayyir al-Dabarān), meaning 'the bright one of the follower'.
  • Location:Aldebaran is located in the zodiac constellation of Taurus and is one of the easiest stars to find in the night sky.
  • Brightness:It varies in brightness from about 0.75 to 0.95, making it the brightest star in the Taurus constellation and one of the brightest in the night sky.
  • Distance:Aldebaran is approximately 65.3 light-years away from Earth.
  • Star Type:It's a red giant star, which means it's cooler than our Sun but much larger, with a surface temperature of 3,910 Kelvin.
  • Mass:Aldebaran is about 16% more massive than the Sun.
  • Size:It has a diameter about 44 times that of the Sun, which is approximately 61 million kilometers.
  • Variability:Aldebaran is a slightly variable star, with a small range of brightness fluctuations.
  • Proper Motion:Over the past 2,000 years, Aldebaran has shifted about 7 minutes of arc in the sky.
  • Asterism:It's a part of an asterism and is often used as a reference star for other celestial objects.
  • Observation:Aldebaran has been extensively studied and used as a benchmark star for various astronomical missions and instruments, including Gaia and the Hubble Space Telescope.

Compared to our Sun

Aldebaran is a red giant star that stands out for its enormous size, with a diameter about 44 times greater than that of our Sun. It's also more massive, containing about 16% more material. However, Aldebaran's surface temperature is notably cooler, around 3,910 Kelvin, in contrast to the Sun's higher temperature of approximately 5,500 degrees Celsius.

What truly sets Aldebaran apart is its tremendous luminosity, shining with about 518 times the brightness of our Sun. In contrast, our Sun is a G-type main-sequence star, primarily converting hydrogen into helium through nuclear fusion, making it less massive, smaller, and hotter than Aldebaran.

Aldebaran, as a giant star, has a lower surface gravity than our Sun. Its surface gravity is about 1.59 centimeters per second squared (cgs). This is typical for a giant star but much lower than Earth's gravity and significantly lower than the Sun's gravity.

The surface of Aldebaran shows specific abundances of elements, including carbon, oxygen, andnitrogen. These abundance patterns suggest that Aldebaran has gone through its first dredge-up stage, which is a natural step in the evolution of a star into a red giant. During this stage, material from the star's core is brought up to its surface by convection.

Location & spoting

Aldebaran is located in the zodiac constellation of Taurus. It can be found in the night sky as part of the Taurus constellation, which is one of the 12 constellations of the zodiac. To be more specific, you can locate Aldebaran by following the three stars of Orion's belt in the direction opposite to Sirius, and the first bright star you'll encounter is Aldebaran. It's relatively easy to spot, making it a notable celestial object in the sky.

Aldebaran is best seen in the late fall and early winter months, typically between late November and early December. During this time, it's prominent in the eastern evening sky.

  1. Begin by identifying the three bright stars in a row that make up Orion's Belt. Orion is a well-known constellation, often visible during the winter months.
  2. Extend an imaginary line through Orion's Belt in the direction away from the constellation Orion, specifically in the opposite direction from the bright star Sirius. Aldebaran is the first bright star you'll encounter along this line.
  3. Aldebaran is usually one of the brightest stars in the sky. It has a reddish appearance, which can help you distinguish it from other nearby stars.

Mythology

The star Aldebaran has an interesting history in different cultures and myths:

  1. In Arabic, it was originally called "نَيِّر اَلدَّبَرَان" (Nayyir al-Dabarān), which means 'the bright one of the follower' because it appears to follow the Pleiades. Sometimes, the Arabs also used this name for the Hyades cluster as a whole. The name Aldebaran, as we know it today, became standard relatively recently.
  2. Aldebaran has been a part of various myths and stories:
    • In Mexican culture, the Seris of northwestern Mexico see Aldebaran as the light for the seven women giving birth, which are represented by the Pleiades. Aldebaran has different names in their culture, including Hant Caalajc Ipápjö, Queeto, and Azoj Yeen oo Caap ('star that goes ahead'). They even have a lunar month named after Aldebaran, called Queeto yaao 'Aldebaran's path' in October.
    • In Australian Aboriginal culture, specifically among the indigenous people of the Clarence River in north-eastern New South Wales, Aldebaran is associated with the story of Karambal. Karambal is believed to be an ancestor who stole another man's wife and, as a result, was pursued and eventually hidden in a tree that was set on fire. It is said that Karambal rose to the sky as smoke and became the star Aldebaran.
    • Several different cultures shared the concept of Taurus as the bull, including the ancient Greeks, Egyptians, Babylonians, and Romans. Though some Native American cultures depicted a bison, instead of a bull, and for the Inuit people, a polar bear.
