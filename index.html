The Bull's bloodshot eye

Aldebaran is such an eye-catching luminary in the dark sky, positioned within the boundaries of Taurus constellation. It is one among the easiest stars to locate and known for its reddish hue.

Being a red giant star means that it is much bigger and colder than our Sun. At about 65 light-years away from Earth, Aldebaran looks quite distinct especially between late November and early December. With an apparent magnitude of 0.86, Aldebaran ranks among the fifteen brightest stars at night.

In Arabic language, Aldebaran means "the Follower", because it usually follows the shining cluster of Pleiades when they rise in the dark.

Its interesting story behind numerous traditions and myths has made it one of most well-known astronomical bodies by both ancient and contemporary stargazers.

Red giant's facts

  • Name Origin:The name Aldebaran comes from the Arabic "الدبران" (Nayyir al-Dabarān), meaning 'the bright one of the follower'.
  • Location:Aldebaran is located in the zodiac constellation of Taurus and is one of the easiest stars to find in the night sky.
  • Brightness:It varies in brightness from about 0.75 to 0.95, making it the brightest star in the Taurus constellation and one of the brightest in the night sky.
  • Distance:Aldebaran is approximately 65.3 light-years away from Earth.
  • Star Type:It's a red giant star, which means it's cooler than our Sun but much larger, with a surface temperature of 3,910 Kelvin.
  • Mass:Aldebaran is about 16% more massive than the Sun.
  • Size:It has a diameter about 44 times that of the Sun, which is approximately 61 million kilometers.
  • Variability:Aldebaran is a slightly variable star, with a small range of brightness fluctuations.
  • Proper Motion:Over the past 2,000 years, Aldebaran has shifted about 7 minutes of arc in the sky.
  • Asterism:It's a part of an asterism and is often used as a reference star for other celestial objects.
  • Observation:Aldebaran has been extensively studied and used as a benchmark star for various astronomical missions and instruments, including Gaia and the Hubble Space Telescope.

Compared to our Sun

A red giant star like Aldebaran is known for its immense size, which has a diameter roughly 44 times larger than the Sun. Additionally it is more massive containing approximately 16% more mass than the Sun. However, one might notice that Aldebaran has a lower surface temperature of around 3910°K compared to about 5500°C for our Sun (thus hotter).

The most remarkable thing about Aldebaran is its luminosity; it shines with brightness far exceeding that of our star by about 518 times. On the other hand, Sun is G-type main-sequence star whose primary nuclear fusion process involves turning hydrogen into helium; making it smaller, less massive, hotter than Aldebaran. As a result, it has lower surface gravity than our own sun because of being a giant star.

Its surface gravity is roughly 1.59 centimeters per second squared (cgs). This value supports the notion for giant stars but is quite small when contrasted against earth's own g-force, let alone that of the sun.

The abundance of elements on the surface of Aldebaran includes carbon, oxygen and nitrogen among others. The observed pattern suggests that the star has experienced its first dredge-up stage. During this stage, material from the star's core is brought up to its surface by convection. It is the natural step in the evolution of a star into a red giant.

Location & spoting

Aldebaran is a star located in Taurus, which is one of the twelve zodiac signs. Being part of Taurus constellation, it lets you see this prominent star in the night sky. To specify, if you track the three stars which represent his belt in Orion constellation but go towards a different path from Sirius then you will easily find Aldebaran as the first bright star.

Because it's easy to recognize, many people consider it an important object of the celestial sphere. Aldebaran can be best viewed during late autumn and early winter season; generally between the beginning and middle of December. At this time, its brilliance is mostly apparent in the eastern side of the sky after sunset during winter months.

  1. The first step is to find out three bright stars that form a line in Orion's Belt. During winter months Orion can be easily identified but there are many constellations that resemble it.
  2. After you've identified these stars, extend an imaginary line through Orion's Belt starting from the belt itself going away from the constellation Orion opposite to the bright star Sirius.
  3. Along this pathway there would be a bright star -Aldebaran- which often happens to be one of the brightest stars around here too. This reddish color can sometimes help you separate it from other surrounding stars.

Mythology

Aldebaran is a star that has undergone interesting changes in various cultures and myths:

  • Originally, in Arabic, it was called نَيِّر اَلدَّبَرَان (Nayyir al-Dabarān), meaning "the bright one of the follower", since it appears to follow the Pleiades. Additionally, the Arabs sometimes used this name for the Hyades cluster. The modern name Aldebaran became common quite lately.
  • In Mexican culture, the Seris of northwestern Mexico see Aldebaran as the light for the seven women giving birth, who are represented by the Pleiades themselves. The different names given to Aldebaran by them include Hant Caalajc Ipápjö, Queeto or Azoj Yeen oo Caap ("star that goes ahead"). They even noticed lunar month called Queeto yaao ("Aldebaran's path") in October after it.
  • Associated with the story of Karambal, Aldebaran is part of Australian Aboriginal culture, especially among the indigenous people of Clarence River- Northeast New South Wales. According to this tale, Karambal was an ancestor who kidnapped another man's wife and was soon followed by the other man until he went into hiding in a tree set ablaze. It is said that Karambal rose as smoke towards heaven where he became the star Aldebaran.
  • The concept of Taurus as a bull was partaken among several cultures such as those of ancient Greeks, Egyptians Babylonians Romans among others while some Native Americans depicted it as a bison whereas for Inuit people it resembled polar bear instead.
